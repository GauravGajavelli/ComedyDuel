# S02E09 — "The Deal" — women trying on clothes — hold-it-up gesture vs. male equivalent

I've been watching women in the department stores.
They're trying on clothes, and I've noticed that they do it differently from men.
Women don't try on the clothes, they get behind the clothes, you see?
They take a dress off the rack, and they hold it up against themself.
They can tell something from this.

They stick one leg way out 'cause they need to know, if some day I'm one-legged, and at a forty-five degree angle, what am I gonna wear?

You never see a man do that.
You never see a guy take a suit off the rack, put his head in the neck, and go, "What do you think about this suit?
I think I'll get it.
Yeah, it looks fine.

Put some shoes by the bottom of the pants, I wanna make sure.
Yeah, perfect.

And what if I'm walking?
Move the shoes, move the shoes, move the shoes, move the shoes.

---

## Agent laugh-points (from annotation, for comparison)
1. "They can tell something from this" — articulation (names shared male bafflement at what information could be extracted from holding a dress against a clothed body)
2. "if some day I'm one-legged, and at a forty-five degree angle, what am I gonna wear?" — reinterpretation / figurative_to_literal (the practical evaluative leg-out pose is reread as literal preparation for a specific hypothetical disability)
3. "put his head in the neck... What do you think about this suit? I think I'll get it." — extension / comparison (the women's hold-it-up premise applied to men produces the absurd image of a head stuck in a suit neck on the rack; act-out of earnest man unaware of absurdity)
4. "Put some shoes by the bottom of the pants, I wanna make sure. Yeah, perfect." — extension / escalation (the act-out escalates: man now wants shoes placed at the bottom to accessorize the headless suit evaluation)
5. "Move the shoes, move the shoes, move the shoes, move the shoes." — extension / escalation + reductio (routine closer; man manually animates shoes to simulate walking; four-beat repetition is the rhythmic payoff and the absurd-but-logical endpoint of the entire routine)
